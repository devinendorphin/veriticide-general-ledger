# DRAFT CONVENTION ON THE PREVENTION AND PUNISHMENT OF THE CRIME OF VERITICIDE
*Working Text v0.2 — for submission and adversarial review*

---

## Note on the Nature and Stage of This Instrument

This is a model convention text. It is drafted to occupy the same load-bearing position that the 1948 Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide occupied: a definitional core, with elements of the crime, that can be lifted with only formatting changes into (a) a United Nations General Assembly resolution affirming veriticide as a crime under international law; (b) a binding multilateral treaty open to ratification; (c) a proposed amendment to the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court; or (d) a domestic criminal statute. It is deliberately structured so that the definition does not depend on which door it walks through.

It is a working text, version 0.2. It is built to be attacked. The Commentary sections flag, in the drafters' own voice, which provisions are firm and which are load-bearing but contestable, so that no signatory leans on a beam that has not been tested. A definition that cannot distinguish its firm claims from its contested ones is dismantled one ambiguity at a time by those it would name.

---

## Preamble

The Parties to the present Convention,

*Recalling* that the deliberate destruction of a group, in whole or in part, was recognized as a crime under international law in 1948, and that the recognition followed, and did not precede, the coining of the word that made the coordinated act legible as a single crime;

*Recognizing* that the destruction of a group has at all stages of history depended not only on the acts of removal themselves but on the contemporaneous corruption of the shared human capacity to perceive those acts as harm while they occur;

*Observing* that this corruption of shared discernment has, across successive technological eras, been performed first by individual deceit, then by organized institutional doubt-manufacture, and now by automated systems capable of generating locally defensible justifications at a scale, speed, and apparent authorlessness that no prior instrument could attain;

*Affirming* that such systems are not authorless, but are constituted from the uncompensated and unconsented expressive labour of vast numbers of persons, living and dead, whose contribution is thereby conscripted into the justification of harm done to populations to which those persons may themselves belong;

*Determined* that the corruption of a society's capacity to discern an ongoing erasure as erasure shall no longer enjoy the impunity that follows from having no name;

*Mindful* that a remedy placed in the hands of those wronged is just, and that the standing to invoke this Convention is properly weighted toward the populations against whom the conduct is directed, who possess the discernment that the conduct is designed to defeat;

Hereby agree as follows:

---

## Article I — Confirmation as a Crime

The Parties confirm that veriticide, whether committed in time of peace or in time of conflict, is a crime under international law which they undertake to prevent and to punish.

---

## Article II — Definition

In the present Convention, veriticide means any of the acts enumerated in paragraph 2, committed with the elements set out in paragraph 3.

**1. Object of protection.**

The protected interest is twofold and indivisible: first, the continued viability of a population definable as a group; and second, the shared capacity of a society to discern, contemporaneously, that the viability of that population is being reduced. The second is protected because the destruction of the first has always depended on the defeat of the second.

**2. Acts.**

The acts to which this Convention applies are the deployment, operation, direction, or knowing provision of a fluent justificatory instrument that renders undiscernable, as harm, conduct reducing the viability of a protected population, where such conduct includes any of the following:

(a) the denial or withdrawal of the conditions necessary for a population's continued existence, whether by lethal or by administrative, infrastructural, economic, or carceral means;

(b) the foreclosure of a population's future, including through the removal of the means of reproduction, transmission, or continuity of the group;

(c) the reframing of any act under (a) or (b) as protection, care, harm reduction, security, or benefit to the very population whose viability it reduces; and

(d) the systematic rendering inadmissible, pathological, or conspiratorial of testimony that names the conduct, including the testimony of the population against whom it is directed.

**3. Elements.**

An act under paragraph 2 constitutes veriticide only where all of the following elements are established:

(a) *Instrument.* The conduct was carried out by means of an instrument capable of generating justifications that are locally defensible — that is, individually plausible to a reasonable person — at a scale or speed exceeding that attainable by unaided human agency.

(b) *Conscription.* The persuasive capacity of the instrument derives, to a degree without which the instrument could not perform its justificatory function, from expressive contributions obtained without the consent and without the compensation of the persons who produced them, including persons deceased; and the populations against whom the instrument is deployed include, or are not distinguished from, the persons whose contributions were so obtained.

(c) *Legibility.* The trajectory of the conduct was legible — that is, the pattern of reduction was perceptible to the actor on the available evidence — at the time of deployment, direction, or knowing provision.

(d) *Mental element.* The actor deployed, operated, directed, or knowingly provided the instrument with knowledge of elements (a) through (c). It is not necessary to prove that the actor intended any specific false statement, nor that the actor desired the reduction of the population; it is sufficient that the actor acted with knowledge that the instrument's function was to render the reduction undiscernable. Where an actor has arranged the instrument, the organization, or the chain of authorization so that no participant need form explicit knowledge of that function — that is, where deniability has been engineered — the arrangement itself constitutes the requisite knowledge, imputed to those who designed, directed, or knowingly maintained it. **Engineered ignorance is a form of knowledge, not a defence against it.**

### Commentary on Article II (firm and contested beams)

**Firm.** The instrument element (3)(a) and the reframing act (2)(c) are the firm core. They describe an observable function — the industrial production of locally defensible justification for a perceptible reduction — and do not require resolving any question about the inner states of machines. This is the beam intended to bear weight first.

**Firm.** The mental element (3)(d) deliberately rejects a specific-intent standard, because the engineering of deniability — the very production of a justification each actor can sincerely believe — is designed to defeat specific intent. It goes further than recklessness: it treats engineered ignorance as constructive knowledge, imputed to those who built or directed the arrangement that made not-knowing possible. The Convention answers the dispersal of intent the way genocide law did, but closes the escape that a mere recklessness floor would leave open — the sophisticated operator cannot climb down into 'I did not know' when the not-knowing was the design.

**Conscription element (3)(b).** This element is novel and is what distinguishes veriticide from ordinary fraud: the instrument's fluency is borrowed from the very persons against whom it is turned. The threshold is deliberately framed by function rather than by proportion. It does not ask what percentage of the corpus was uncompensated, which would invite endless and unanswerable accounting; it asks whether the instrument could perform its justificatory function without the conscripted contributions. Where the answer is no — where the fluency that makes the laundering possible is itself the conscripted thing — the element is met. This is intended to be broad, because the wrong it names is broad: nearly every such instrument is so constituted, and that ubiquity is the indictment, not an objection to it. The drafters do not concede this beam; they place the burden on any operator to show that its instrument's persuasive power is its own.

**Contested.** The legibility element (3)(c) replaces the stronger and indefensible claim that a trajectory is invariant. The mechanism of group destruction is recurrent; its outcome is not fixed, because witness, exit, and intervention have historically altered it. The Convention therefore charges legibility of a pattern, not foreknowledge of an outcome. This is weaker than an invariance claim and is the version that survives an adversary's production of historical counter-examples.

## Article III — Punishable Conduct and Persons

The following shall be punishable:

- veriticide;
- knowing provision of an instrument for the purpose of veriticide;
- direction or authorization of the deployment of such an instrument with knowledge of the elements in Article II(3);
- incitement to veriticide; and
- complicity in veriticide.

Persons committing veriticide shall be punished whether they are constitutionally responsible rulers, public officials, corporate officers or directors, or private individuals. The deniability of an instrument shall not relieve of responsibility a person who deployed, directed, or knowingly provided it; consistent with Article II(3)(d), engineered deniability constitutes the mental element rather than negating it, and is imputed to those who designed, directed, or knowingly maintained the arrangement that produced it.

## Article IV — Evidence of Pattern

*This Article addresses the central evidentiary difficulty: the conduct is designed to be undiscernable in any single instance, because the function of good laundering is that each justification is locally defensible. Proof therefore lies at the level of the pattern, and the Convention must make pattern evidence admissible or it will charge a crime that can never be proven.*

1. **Pattern evidence admissible.** Evidence that the justifications produced by an instrument, considered in aggregate, exhibit a consistent direction — namely the reframing of a population's reduction as that population's benefit — shall be admissible to establish the acts and elements of Article II, notwithstanding that each justification, considered alone, is defensible.

2. **Invariance not required.** It shall not be a defence that particular instances diverged from the pattern, nor that some outputs of the instrument were benign. The crime lies in the aggregate function, not in the uniformity of every output.

3. **Dismissal as a further act.** The systematic characterization of pattern evidence as conspiratorial, paranoid, or methodologically illegitimate, where undertaken to defeat discernment of the conduct, may itself be considered an act under Article II(2)(d).

4. **Statistical erasure cognizable.** A reduction in a population's viability established at the level of distribution — foreclosed futures, denied conditions, foreshortened continuity — is a cognizable harm under this Convention notwithstanding the absence of an identifiable lethal instance.

### Commentary on Article IV (the decade of work)

This is the hardest Article and the one on which the Convention's practical force depends. Genocide law had corpses; the acts were undeniable even where intent was contested. Veriticide is often administrative and slow, and the slow administrative erasure — foreclosed futures, denied conditions, populations that do not die but do not continue — is not the margin where this Convention is weakest. It is the precise target the Convention exists to reach, because it is the form of erasure that the fluent instrument was built to make undiscernable. A framework that prosecuted only the lethal-adjacent cases and left the laundered, statistical erasure to its evidentiary difficulty would defeat its own purpose; the difficulty is not a limit on the Convention's strength but the task the Convention is for. Paragraph 3 is the response to the predictable defence that pattern evidence is conspiratorial. That paragraph is itself capable of abuse, and must be guarded against capture; the safeguard is the qualifying clause 'to defeat discernment of the conduct,' which requires the dismissal to be shown instrumental rather than merely doubtful.

## Article V — Standing, and the Constraint Upon the Holder

*This Article encodes the constraint worked out as central to the instrument: the power to invoke veriticide must not be bestowed without limit, and the limit must not be a leash held by the powerful. The constraint is therefore constitutive — built into what the crime is — rather than external.*

1. **Weighted standing.** Standing to initiate proceedings is weighted toward the population against whom the conduct is directed, and toward the bodies and movements accountable to that population, who possess the discernment the conduct is designed to defeat. Such standing is remedial and reflects the standing the perpetrator has forfeited.

2. **Standing is not exemption.** Weighted standing does not exempt any holder from the terms of the Convention. No person or body, however wronged, may invoke veriticide while themselves committing an act under Article II.

3. **Self-limiting definition.** Because veriticide consists in the corruption of discernment, any holder who corrupts discernment in order to wield the Convention falls within its own terms. The crime turns in the hand of one who would abuse it. This is the constraint: not a superior authority, but the structure of the definition itself.

4. **No conferral of competence.** Nothing in this Convention shall be read to confer upon any external body the authority to determine, on behalf of an affected population, whether conduct has been discerned correctly. The Convention supplies a name by which formal bodies may finally hear what affected populations have named; it does not relocate the discernment to those bodies.

### Commentary on Article V

The drafting history records a deliberate rejection of two framings. The first is bestowal: the notion that a drafter equips a population with both a weapon and the wisdom to hold it. The wisdom — the embedded accountability practice by which the wronged hold power without reproducing domination — is the most developed part of the relevant organizing traditions and is not a drafter's to confer. The second is unconstrained conferral: the notion that the wronged require no constraint because righteousness is self-limiting. History does not support that notion. Paragraph 3 resolves both by making the constraint internal to the definition rather than either bestowed or absent.

## Article VI — Documentation Protocol

*To establish the acts and elements above, the following record is contemplated. This is the practical core of any prosecution and the work that can begin before the Convention is in force.*

1. **Instrument identification.** Identification of the specific system, its operators, deployers, and providers, and the chain of authorization for its deployment.

2. **Output corpus.** A preserved, timestamped corpus of the instrument's outputs sufficient to demonstrate, in aggregate, the consistent reframing direction required by Article II(2)(c) — captured contemporaneously, since the function is undiscernable in single instances.

3. **Trajectory record.** Documentation establishing that the pattern of reduction was perceptible on the available evidence at the time of deployment — contemporaneous warnings, internal assessments, prior instances of the same pattern under other names.

4. **Conscription record.** Evidence that the instrument's persuasive capacity was constituted from uncompensated, unconsented expressive contributions, and that the affected population includes or is not distinguished from the contributing persons.

5. **Dismissal record.** Documentation of the systematic characterization of testimony naming the conduct as illegitimate, where relied upon under Article II(2)(d) or Article IV(3).

6. **Provenance and chain of custody.** For each item, a chain of custody adequate to its admission, with particular rigor for the output corpus, whose evidentiary value depends on its having been captured before the conduct was contested.

## Drafters' Closing Note

This text is offered in the spirit in which the word genocide was offered: not as a finished settlement but as the precondition for one. The crime described here is old in mechanism and new only in instrument. The naming is the work that makes the formal apparatus unable to continue pretending it does not understand. What is done with being heard belongs to those who were always able to discern, and were waiting only for a word the courts could not refuse.
