# DOCUMENTATION & STANDING PROTOCOL
*For Recording Atrocity Laundering While It Is Ongoing*

Companion to the Declaration on Veriticide and the Convention on Instrumental Veriticide · Usable independently · v0.1

---

## 0. What This Protocol Is, and Who It Is For

**Purpose.** This Protocol lets a person or group create a record of harm-laundering that will hold up later — before any court recognizes the offense, before any law is passed, and even if none ever is. It is built so that the record is useful in three situations at once: as evidence if a forum eventually exists; as a public account that resists the "each instance was reasonable" defense even with no forum; and as a working tool for the people closest to the harm to confirm to themselves and each other that the pattern they are seeing is real.

**Who uses it.** Frontline workers, community organizations, archivists, clinicians, journalists, librarians, public defenders, affected individuals, and independent researchers. No legal training is assumed. No permission is required. You do not need anyone to agree that a crime is occurring in order to begin recording.

**The one idea underneath everything here.** The harm being recorded is designed to be invisible one piece at a time. Each individual output, decision, memo, or denial looks defensible on its own. The harm only becomes visible in the aggregate — in the direction all the pieces point together, over time. Therefore the single most important thing this Protocol asks you to do is capture the pieces as they happen, with their dates intact, before anyone has reason to contest them. A pattern reconstructed after the fact is weak. A pattern preserved as it formed is strong.

**Instrument-agnostic.** This Protocol applies to any system that produces, ranks, distributes, or authorizes explanations and decisions at scale: an AI model or chatbot, a recommender system, an automated eligibility or risk-scoring system, a press or communications office, a policy-issuance process, a triage protocol. It is not specific to artificial intelligence. Where the instrument is AI-based, an additional track (Section 5) applies, but the core record does not depend on it.

## 1. Before You Begin: Documenter Safety

Recording this kind of pattern can carry risk, especially for people inside the institutions doing the harm and for members of the affected population. Read this section first. None of the rest of the Protocol is worth a documenter being endangered.

**Separate yourself from the record.** Where possible, store the record somewhere that is not tied to your name, your work account, or a device that can be seized or remotely wiped. The record should be able to survive you losing access to it.

**Protect sources and the affected.** Record what was done and by what role or office, not necessarily by what named individual, unless naming is safe and consented. A pattern is provable from roles and outputs; it does not require exposing a frightened witness.

**Assume the record may be read by the people it documents.** Write nothing in it you could not defend as accurate. Speculation, anger, and analysis belong in a separate file from the factual record (see Section 3).

**Know your obligations and your protections.** Whistleblower protections, mandatory-reporting duties, confidentiality rules, and data-protection law vary by place and role. This Protocol does not override them. When in doubt, consult someone who knows your jurisdiction before disclosing externally.

**Collective is safer than solo.** A record held by an accountable group, with more than one custodian, is harder to discredit, harder to suppress, and less dangerous to any single person than a record held alone.

## 2. The Record: Six Tracks

A complete record has six tracks. You do not need all six to begin, and a record missing some tracks is still valuable. But the more tracks you preserve, the harder the record is to dismiss. Track A is the spine; start there.

| Track | What it captures | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **A — Instrument & Outputs** | What the system is, and a timestamped sample of what it actually produced or decided. | The spine. Shows the aggregate direction that single instances hide. |
| **B — Authorization** | Who deployed, ran, directed, or kept the system running, and the chain of approval. | Connects the pattern to decision-makers, not just the machine. |
| **C — Trajectory & Warnings** | Evidence the direction was perceptible at the time: warnings, audits, dissent, prior instances. | Defeats "we could not have known." |
| **D — Dismissal & Retaliation** | How testimony naming the pattern was suppressed, buried, or punished — and by whom. | Records the second-order harm: the defeat of discernment itself. |
| **E — Conscription (if present)** | Evidence the system's fluency was built from uncompensated expression of the affected group. | Optional for documentation-record purposes — Track A stands without it. Note: Convention Art. II(3)(b) lists conscription as a required element for criminal liability; a record lacking this track documents the pattern but does not independently establish all Convention elements. |
| **F — Custody** | How each item was captured, stored, and kept unaltered. | Makes the whole record believable and usable later. |

## 3. Track A — Instrument and Outputs (the Spine)

**Identify the instrument.** In plain language, record: what the system is, what it is called, who makes it, who runs it, what it is used to decide or produce, and over what population or caseload. One paragraph is enough to start.

**Capture outputs contemporaneously.** This is the central act of the whole Protocol. Each time the system produces an output, decision, denial, score, or explanation that bears on the population, capture it as it happens. For each captured item, record six fields:

1. Date and time of the output (as exact as you can get).
2. The exact output — quote or screenshot the wording, score, or decision verbatim; do not paraphrase.
3. The input or situation that produced it (what was asked, who the subject was, what triggered it).
4. The stated justification — the reason the system or operator gave, in its own words.
5. The actual effect on the person or group.
6. How you captured it (screenshot, export, transcription, copy of a letter).

**Why verbatim matters.** The whole mechanism you are documenting works by making each output individually defensible. If you paraphrase, you hand the operator the argument that you have distorted it. The exact wording is what later lets a reader see the inversion — the moment the reduction of a population is described as protection of, care for, or benefit to that same population.

**Watch for the six laundering moves.** When you log the stated justification, note which of these it does. This taxonomy is what turns a pile of outputs into a legible pattern:

1. **Concealment** — the conduct is hidden or not disclosed.
2. **Denial** — the conduct is said not to be happening.
3. **Fragmentation** — the conduct is broken into isolated events so no one sees the whole.
4. **Reframing as benefit** — the harm is described as care, protection, security, treatment, necessity, or neutrality toward the very population it harms. *(This is the core move. Flag it wherever it appears.)*
5. **Attribution to the victim** — the harm is blamed on the population's own choices, pathology, noncompliance, or risk.
6. **Discrediting testimony** — those who name the pattern are cast as paranoid, extreme, incompetent, or conspiratorial.

**Relationship to the Ledger Analytical Taxonomy.** The six moves above are the *Harm Documentation Taxonomy* (v0.1): they describe observable structural and behavioral patterns used to organize field documentation of what an institution did. The Veriticide Master Ledger uses a separate *Veriticide Analytical Taxonomy* (v0.1): six epistemic and rhetorical moves — care-register reframing, self-evidence assertion, disqualification of dissent, unfalsifiable overlay, euphemism/bureaucratic abstraction, benefit reframe — that describe how justificatory language operates to defeat recognition. These schemas are complementary, not competing, and should not be treated as alternative encodings of the same six slots. The Protocol taxonomy answers: *what did the institution do?* The Ledger taxonomy answers: *how did the language work to make it unrecognizable?* Field workers logging raw outputs use this taxonomy. Formatted Ledger entries use the analytical taxonomy. Both are explicitly versioned as distinct frameworks.

**Keep analysis separate from record.** Your reading of what the pattern means is valuable, but keep it in a separate analysis file. The factual record — dates, outputs, effects — must be able to stand on its own, so that someone who distrusts your conclusions can still not dispute your facts.

## 4. Tracks B, C, and D

### Track B — Authorization

Record who is responsible, by role wherever naming individuals is unsafe. The aim is to connect the pattern to the people who chose to deploy or continue the system, since a machine cannot be the only thing in the dock.

- Who built or supplied the system; who deployed it; who operates it day to day.
- The chain of approval — who authorized its use over this population, and when.
- Any point where someone had the power to stop or change it and did not.
- Any restructuring that appears designed so that no single person has to know what the system does as a whole — record this specifically; it is itself significant.

### Track C — Trajectory and Warnings

This track defeats the defense that the harm could not have been foreseen. You are showing the direction was perceptible at the time — not that the outcome was certain, only that it was visible to anyone looking.

- Contemporaneous warnings: internal memos, dissent, complaints, resignations.
- Audits, reviews, or assessments — especially ones that were commissioned and then ignored or buried.
- External reports, news, academic or community findings available at the time.
- Prior instances of the same pattern under other names or in other places — the recurrence is part of the legibility.

State the trajectory as a perceptible pattern, never as an inevitable outcome. "The direction was visible and deployment continued" is provable and survives challenge. "The outcome was certain" is not, and a single counter-example discredits it.

### Track D — Dismissal and Retaliation

This track records the defeat of discernment itself: what happened to people who named the pattern. Record conduct, not mere disagreement. Honest skepticism is not what this track captures; interested suppression by actors with power over the evidentiary field is.

Capture where an actor with control over evidence, funding, access, ranking, publication, or service did any of the following in order to prevent recognition of the pattern:

- Suppressed, destroyed, or withheld evidence or records.
- Falsified or materially misrepresented the evidence or the pattern.
- Flooded or buried the signal — algorithmically, procedurally, or through volume.
- Retaliated against witnesses, analysts, workers, or members of the affected population who spoke.
- Encoded dismissal into a system — building "this population's reports are unreliable" into triage, ranking, eligibility, funding, or legitimacy.

The distinction that keeps this track honest and usable: a critic saying "that seems paranoid" is not what you are recording. An actor with power over the evidentiary field acting to make the pattern unseeable is. Record the position and the power, not just the words.

## 5. Track E — Conscription (Optional for Documentation; Required for Convention Criminal Elements)

Capture this track only where the instrument is AI-based and the information is available. For documentation-record purposes, it is an aggravating track: the record is complete and usable without it. However, Convention Art. II(3)(b) treats conscription as a required element for criminal liability — a record lacking this track documents the pattern but does not independently establish all elements the Convention requires for prosecution. Its significance when present is that it documents the deepest version of the harm: a system whose persuasive fluency was built from the uncompensated expression of people — living and dead — then used to make the disposal of people like them sound reasonable.

- What is known or claimed about the system's training data — sources, scale, consent, compensation.
- Whether the affected population's own expression (their writing, speech, images, records, cultural material) is plausibly part of that data.
- Any model documentation, model card, public statement, or disclosure bearing on data provenance.
- Whether the operator has resisted disclosure of training data — the resistance is itself worth recording.

Do not overclaim here. Record what is known and what is merely plausible, clearly distinguished. The strength of this track is honesty about its own limits; an overclaimed conscription record weakens the whole file.

## 6. Track F — Custody (What Makes the Record Believable)

A record that cannot be shown to be unaltered can be dismissed no matter how true it is. Custody is the least glamorous track and the one that most determines whether the record survives contact with people who want it discredited.

- **Timestamp at capture.** Record the date and time you captured each item, separately from the date the item itself bears. Where possible, use a method that makes after-the-fact alteration evident.
- **Preserve the original form.** Keep the screenshot, export, or original file, not just a typed-up version. Keep the original alongside any transcription.
- **Record the capture method.** For each item, note how you got it and from where. "Screenshot of the eligibility portal on [date]" is worth more than an unsourced quote.
- **Use more than one custodian.** Where safe, have the record held by more than one person or organization, so it cannot be lost or suppressed by reaching a single point.
- **Log every change.** If the record is ever corrected or added to, log what changed, when, and by whom. A record with an honest edit history is more credible than one that claims to be untouched.
- **Back up off-platform.** Do not keep the only copy inside a system controlled by the institution you are documenting, or on a single device.

## 7. Standing: What This Record Entitles You to Ask For

This Protocol takes a two-step position on standing, deliberately. The two steps are different and must not be collapsed.

**Step one — the right to act without permission.** Affected populations and the organizations accountable to them do not need a tribunal's prior agreement to recognize, document, preserve, publish, and seek urgent review of a pattern of viability-reducing conduct laundered as care, security, neutrality, or benefit. The competence to name what is happening to you, before authorized experts have blessed it, is not granted by this Protocol — it is presupposed by it. A record built under this Protocol is a basis to demand preservation of evidence, disclosure of how a system is used, independent audit, and provisional restraint.

**Step two — what still requires proof.** Establishing that a specific person or organization is criminally responsible is a different matter, and remains subject to proof before a competent and impartial tribunal, with full procedural rights for the accused. This Protocol does not make anyone guilty. It makes a pattern visible and preserved. Guilt is for a forum that does not yet exist to determine if it ever does.

**Why the two steps are kept apart.** Collapsing them produces the two failures this framework most wants to avoid. Granting institutions full authority over step one repeats the harm procedurally — it makes the affected wait for permission to know what they are seeing, from the same bodies whose recognition is being obstructed. But asserting step two without proof — declaring guilt by accusation — hands the operator the easiest possible rebuttal and discredits the record. The affected population's discernment governs naming, preservation, and the demand for review. A tribunal's proof governs punishment. Both are necessary; neither substitutes for the other.

**The clause that does not narrow away.** No future narrowing of the associated Declaration or Convention, and no institution's preference for a tidier standard, shall be read to deny the competence of affected populations to name, document, and seek urgent review of patterns of reduction before those patterns have been recognized by states, courts, or expert bodies. If recognition must wait for the powerful to agree the harm is occurring, recognition has already failed.

## 8. Quick Start (If You Only Read One Section)

1. **Start the spine today.** Open one file. Each time the system produces an output that bears on the population, log six fields: date, exact output, the input, the stated justification, the real effect, how you captured it. Verbatim, never paraphrased.

2. **Flag the move.** For each, note which of the six laundering moves the justification performs — especially reframing-as-benefit, the core one.

3. **Keep the originals.** Screenshots and exports, off-platform, with capture dates. More than one custodian if you can.

4. **Keep your analysis in a separate file.** Let the facts stand alone so a skeptic cannot dispute them even if they dispute you.

5. **Protect yourself and your sources first.** By role, not name, where naming is unsafe. The record should survive you losing access to it.

**You do not need permission to begin.** No recognized crime, no court, no law is required for this record to be worth making. Its value begins the moment the first dated output is preserved.
