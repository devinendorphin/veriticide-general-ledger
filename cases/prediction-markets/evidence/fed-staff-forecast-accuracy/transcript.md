## A. The claim (granted, then bounded)

- Prediction markets genuinely aggregate dispersed knowledge, and on some questions perform on par
  with or better than expert/survey forecasts. The source material cites a **Federal Reserve staff
  finding** that these markets performed **on par with or better than Bloomberg consensus and survey
  forecasts on rate decisions** (via TS Imagine).
- This is the **CONTROL / good-faith case** the charge theory grants *first*: the price is not
  meaningless, and refusing to grant the genuine information-aggregation virtue would itself be the
  disqualification-of-dissent move pointed the other way (Reflexivity Clause / Art. V).

## B. Why it is in this case

Entry 17's thesis is that **the virtue and the vulnerability are one organ** — the price is valuable
*because* people believe it, which is exactly what turns moving the price into moving the record.
The case therefore *needs* this item on the record: it is the strongest statement of the good-faith
case, against which the laundering structure is measured. It also defines a **falsification lever**
(§B of the memo): if the markets' accuracy advantage holds *and* an enforced exclusion wall keeps
pricers and causers apart, the architecture is closer to protected financial innovation than to the
charged instrument.
