## A. The facts on the record

- Prediction market **Kalshi** fined and suspended (five-year suspensions) **three congressional
  candidates** for trading on their own races during primary campaigns:
  - **Mark Moran** — former Virginia Democratic primary candidate for Senate, now running as an
    independent; said on social media he "traded $100 on myself"; **refused** to reach an agreement
    and was **fined the most, more than $6,200**; told the AP he placed the bets *intending to draw
    attention to the unjust sway platforms like Kalshi have on elections*.
  - **Matt Klein** — Minnesota Democrat running in the primary for the state's 2nd Congressional
    District; placed bets under $100 on his own candidacy; reached an agreement; penalty **over $530**.
  - **Ezekiel Enriquez** — Texas, Republican primary for the 21st Congressional District; placed bets
    under $100 on his own candidacy; reached an agreement; penalty **over $780**.
- Reported as Kalshi "insider trading" enforcement; fines ranged **$539 to more than $6,200**.

## B. Why it is in this case (it cuts two ways)

1. **Partial CONTROL / good-faith fact (logged honestly).** Kalshi has explicit written rules against
   trading on events one can influence, and it *enforced* them here. This is the closest thing the
   record holds to the exclusion wall the falsification memo (§E) demands — and it is logged as a
   point in the architecture's favor, not buried.
2. **Foreseeability + the wall's gap (Art. II(3)(c)).** The same enforcement proves the operators
   *knew* the outcome-channel harm was live. Yet the wall caught only small-dollar candidates betting
   on their own races; it did **not** catch the lever-holder with classified information
   (`vandyke-venezuela-insider-bet-2026`) *ex ante*. A wall that fires on $100 candidate bets but not
   on a $33K classified-insider war bet is a partial, not a sufficient, control — and the destruction-
   skewed contract menu it polices is the one the regulator is re-opening.
