## A. Mass firing of inspectors general (~January 24, 2025)

- On or about **January 24, 2025** — described by the court as **"the fifth day of his second
  term"** — the administration removed **at least 17 Senate-confirmed inspectors general** across
  cabinet departments and agencies.
- IGs were **notified by email**, citing **"changing priorities"** as the reason.
- **No 30-day advance notice to Congress** and **no substantive, case-specific rationale** were
  provided, as required for IG removal under the **Inspector General Act of 1978** as amended by
  the **Securing Inspector General Independence Act of 2022**.
- The **DOJ Inspector General (Michael Horowitz) was not** among those removed (counter-detail).

## B. Litigation and ruling — *Storch v. Hegseth*

- **Filed February 12, 2025** in the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia by **eight**
  fired IGs: **Robert Storch** (Defense), **Michael Missal** (Veterans Affairs), **Christi Grimm**
  (Health and Human Services), **Cardell Richardson, Sr.** (State), **Sandra Bruce** (Education),
  **Phyllis Fong** (Agriculture), **Larry Turner** (Labor), **Hannibal "Mike" Ware** (Small
  Business Administration).
- Case: ***Storch v. Hegseth***, **No. 1:25-cv-00415 (D.D.C.)**.
- **September 24, 2025** — District Judge **Ana C. Reyes** ruled the firings unlawful: it is
  **"obvious"** the administration broke the law by ignoring the requirement to notify Congress
  30 days in advance and to provide the **"substantive rationale, including detailed and
  case-specific reasons"** for the removals.
- The court **declined to reinstate** the IGs: plaintiffs failed to show that being denied the
  30-day notice period constituted irreparable harm, and the President could still lawfully remove
  them after providing the required 30 days' notice to Congress.

## C. Targeted firing — USAID IG Paul Martin (February 11, 2025), one day after a critical report

- **February 11, 2025 (evening):** USAID Inspector General **Paul Martin** (IG since 2023) was
  terminated by email. Deputy director of the Office of Presidential Personnel **Trent Morse**
  wrote: **"your position as Inspector General of the United States Agency for International
  Development is terminated, effective immediately."** A USAID OIG spokesperson confirmed the
  termination and said **no reason was given**.
- The firing came **one day after** his office released a report (Feb 10, 2025) on the impact of
  the administration's curtailment of USAID. The report warned the staffing/oversight gap left the
  agency **"susceptible to inadvertently funding entities or salaries of individuals associated
  with U.S.-designated terrorist organizations,"** and described uncertainty around Secretary of
  State **Marco Rubio's** lifesaving-assistance waivers, which it said put **"more than
  $489 million of food assistance at ports, in transit, and in warehouses at risk of spoilage,
  unanticipated storage needs, and diversion."**

## Evidentiary reading (analyst)

The mass firing establishes unlawful removal of the oversight **corps** (per the *Storch* ruling)
but the stated ground was the generic "changing priorities" — it does **not**, on the public
record, tie each removal to a specific pending review. The **USAID/Martin** removal is the one case
where **foreclosure of a specific review** is supported, by **one-day temporal proximity** between
a published OIG report naming the precise harm (food aid at risk; waiver uncertainty; oversight
gap) and the IG's termination. That single case additionally implicates foreseeability/knowledge
(Convention Art. II(3)(c)/(d)), because the review it foreclosed had already named the harm.
